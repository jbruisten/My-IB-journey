The IB Educator Journey

A side-by-side exploration of how teacher inquiry fuels student growth, and how students, in turn, teach the educator.

A Reciprocal Cycle of Learning

Education is not a top-down transmission; it is a collaborative loop.

Teacher Action

Intentional Scaffolding

The teacher identifies the "tightrope" of the student's Zone of Proximal Development, providing templates and AI-assisted tools (Excel) to lower barriers to entry.

Student Impact

Independent Inquiry

The student gains the confidence to move beyond simple task completion, using the provided scaffolds to ask deeper "Why" questions about their data.

Teacher Action

Relational Trust

Using trauma-sensitive strategies (New Authority), the teacher creates a safe space where students can fail without shame during a chemistry experiment.

Student Impact

Emotional Resilience

The student reflects on anomalies in their work with honesty rather than fear, growing more resilient in the face of scientific uncertainty.

The Student Impact on the Teacher

"When students begin to reflect honestly, they challenge my own assumptions. Their growth teaches me to be a more responsive practitioner. I learn that my routine is not the curriculum—the relationship is. Their successes refine my scaffolding, and their challenges keep my own reflective practice alive."

Module 4: The Reflective Community

Week 1: Community of Learners

Defined by shared responsibility and trust. I align this with Ali Fenwick’s social bonding theories—seeing learning as a collective process rather than top-down transmission.

Week 2: Inquiry Culture

Reflection encourages students to think about how they learn. The 'viva voce' is a vital milestone for students to articulate their evolution as researchers.

Week 3: Socrates

"The unexamined life is not worth living." In science, we continuously test results; I apply this same rigour to my teaching methods.

Week 4: PLC Case Study

Trauma-sensitive teaching in Special Education.

Addressing behavioral masking through consistent relational trust across diverse teaching backgrounds.

Module 4 Reflection

"Module 4 highlighted how important collaboration and trust are, but also how difficult they can be in practice. I recognised that strong relationships don’t automatically lead to effective collaboration—teams can feel safe but still avoid meaningful change. For me, it reinforced that reflection should not stay individual, but needs to be part of honest, sometimes uncomfortable dialogue within a team to really improve practice."

Module 1: Paradigm Shift

Reflection

"Module 1 was my first real introduction to the IB... I initially expected it to be mainly a structured curriculum, but I came to understand that it is much more focused on inquiry, reflection, and developing the whole student. This differs from more traditional, outcome-driven systems like the Dutch 'Profielwerkstuk'. It made me reflect on moving beyond just guiding students to complete tasks, and instead support them in becoming more independent and reflective learners."

Core Vision

"I see the IB philosophy as a system that uses reflection, relationships and inquiry to pursue meaningful learning."
